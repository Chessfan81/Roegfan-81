The Project

Three layers. One story.

This analysis investigates US homicide trends using FBI Supplementary Homicide Reports data (Kaggle, 1980–2014), fused with US Census population data to produce population-adjusted rates. The analysis unfolds in three layers — like Russian dolls — each revealing a story within the broader national picture.

Layer 1 — Statistical

Time series forecasting using ARIMA modelling, validated by Augmented Dickey-Fuller testing, ACF/PACF analysis, and comparison against Holt and Prophet models.

Layer 2 — Sociological

White and Black homicide trends compared — revealing a striking parallel shaped by the crack cocaine epidemic, the 1994 Federal Crime Bill, and systemic macro factors.

The Analysis

Population-adjusted homicide rate

Adjusting for population growth is essential — the US population grew from 230 million in 1980 to 323 million by 2014. Raw counts alone would understate the true scale of the decline. The adjusted rate tells the real story: a fall from 10 homicides per 100,000 people in 1980 to just 4.4 by 2014.

"A notable observation is the near-identical homicide counts in 2013 (14,445) and 2014 (14,331) — suggesting the series may have reached a structural floor by the early 2010s."

Forecasting the trend

Four models were tested: ARIMA(1,1,1), ARIMA(0,1,1), Holt's Linear Trend, and Facebook's Prophet. The series was split at 2000 — training on 1980–2000, predicting 2001–2014. Prophet emerged as the clear winner both visually and on RMSE (0.28), closely mirroring the shape of the actual decline.

The racial convergence

Perhaps the most striking finding in the dataset: White and Black homicide rates follow a nearly identical trajectory. Both rise during the crack cocaine epidemic (1984–1994), both peak around 1991–1993, and both decline steeply thereafter. This is an inherently anti-racist finding — the drivers were systemic and societal, not race-specific.

"The parallel trends point to systemic macro-sociological drivers — drug markets, policing reforms, and economic conditions — operating across racial boundaries simultaneously."

Key Findings

What the data reveals

1

A 55% decline — but not a smooth one

The population-adjusted homicide rate fell from 10 per 100,000 in 1980 to 4.4 by 2014. The decline was not linear — a sharp rise to a 1993 peak (driven by the crack epidemic) preceded the fall, and a plateau emerged from around 2008 onwards.

2

Prophet outperforms ARIMA for this series

Facebook's Prophet model achieved an RMSE of 0.28 — significantly better than ARIMA(1,1,1) at 0.49. Prophet's changepoint detection captured the post-1993 decline trajectory more accurately than autoregressive approaches.

3

Race convergence points to systemic causes

White and Black homicide rates follow a strikingly parallel path. The Black rate briefly exceeded the White rate during 1990–1994 — the peak of the crack cocaine epidemic — before converging. This finding is anti-racist in its implication: macro-societal forces, not race-specific factors, drove both trends.

4

The decline is geographically concentrated

California, New York, Texas and Michigan drove the national decline. Southern states including Georgia show flat or rising rankings — Georgia moved from 10th to 5th place between 1980 and 2014, bucking the national trend.

5

Intersectionality — every category hides another story

National → racial → geographic disaggregation reveals increasingly nuanced patterns. Sex, age, weapon type, and perpetrator-victim relationship each contain further layers waiting to be explored — the Russian dolls principle applied to crime data.
